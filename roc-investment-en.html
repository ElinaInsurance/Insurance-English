06 / How It Works

ROC Tax Mechanics:
Why the CRA Treats It Differently

The CRA classifies Return of Capital as "your own money returned to you" — not investment income. So it's not taxable when received.

📋
T3 Slip Box 42 — The Tax Treatment
ROC distributions appear in Box 42 of your T3 slip — information only. Not included in your taxable income for the year received. They reduce your Adjusted Cost Base (ACB) instead. Tax is settled only when you sell.
🧮
Why the Exit Tax Rate Is So Much Lower
Regular distributions: 30% income tax — paid every year at full rate

ROC at exit: capital gains tax = gain × 50% inclusion × 30% = ~15% effective rate

Same dollars, but the exit tax rate is half the regular rate — AND you've benefited from 10 years of tax-deferred compounding on the deferred amount.
🔑 The Simple Framing
ROC is "spend it now, pay a lower bill later." The money that would have gone to annual taxes instead stays in your pool compounding at 13%. After 10 years, you settle up at 15% — half the rate you'd have paid annually. The math is unambiguous.
07 / Key Considerations

ROC Is Tax Deferral,
Not Tax Elimination

✅ ROC Advantages
  • Zero tax at distribution
  • More capital compounds longer
  • Exit rate is only 15%
  • No contribution cap
  • Full cash flow available
  • Pairs with capital loss carryforwards
⚠️ Watch For
  • Lower ACB = larger exit gain
  • Must track cumulative ACB manually
  • ACB at zero triggers tax again
  • Schedule 3 requires manual ACB
  • ROC ≠ investment quality signal
  • Requires advisor coordination
