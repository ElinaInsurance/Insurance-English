01 / The Basics

What Is
Corporate-Owned Life Insurance?

Business owners have two options for owning life insurance: personal ownership or corporate ownership. Corporate-Owned Life Insurance (COLI) is an arrangement where the corporation is the policyholder, premium payer, and typically also the beneficiary.

🏢
How It Works
The corporation pays premiums using low-tax active business income (small business tax rate ~15%). Since life insurance premiums are generally not tax-deductible, using corporate funds is significantly more efficient than paying from after-personal-tax income.
💡
Tracy's Classic Example
Annual premium: $10,000. Tracy's personal marginal tax rate: 50%. Corporate small business rate: 15%.

• Personal ownership: Corp must earn $20,000 pre-tax to deliver $10,000 after-tax to Tracy
• Corporate ownership: Corp only needs to earn $11,765 pre-tax to pay the $10,000 premium directly

Annual savings of $8,235 in pre-tax income!
🔑 The Simple Formula
COLI = Low-Tax Premium Savings + Tax-Exempt Cash Value Growth + CDA Tax-Free Estate Transfer

04 / The Tax Engine

The CDA Mechanism:
Tax-Free Estate Transfer

The most powerful tax advantage of COLI lies in the Capital Dividend Account (CDA) — a unique tax-free channel granted by Canadian tax law exclusively to private corporations.

📐 CDA Flow Process
1
Shareholder dies → Corporation receives death benefit proceeds
2
Proceeds − Adjusted Cost Basis (ACB) = CDA credit added
3
Corporation files CRA Form T2054 → Approval to pay capital dividend
4
Shareholders (estate/spouse/heirs) receive completely tax-free capital dividend ✓
📋
What Is the ACB?
The Adjusted Cost Basis (ACB) = cumulative premiums paid − Net Cost of Pure Insurance (NCPI). Over time, NCPI gradually reduces the ACB toward zero. When ACB reaches zero, the full death benefit becomes the CDA credit — meaning 100% tax-free estate transfer.
⚠️ Critical Structuring Rule
The CDA mechanism requires the corporation to be both policyholder and beneficiary. If a shareholder is named as beneficiary, the premiums paid by the corporation may be treated as a taxable shareholder benefit, eliminating all tax advantages.

06 / Living Benefits

Access While Alive:
Three Cash Value Strategies

Corporate tax-exempt life insurance isn't only a death benefit — during the policy's lifetime, accumulated cash values can provide flexible funding for personal or business needs.

💸
Option 1
Partial or Full Cash Withdrawal
🏦
Option 2
Policy Loan from Insurer
🔗
Option 3
Collateral Assignment Bank Loan
⚠️
Options 1 & 2: Withdrawal or Policy Loan
Both trigger a deemed disposition that may result in a taxable policy gain, treated as passive corporate income. Suitable for short-term cash needs but tax implications must be carefully evaluated first.
⭐
Option 3 (Most Recommended): Collateral Assignment
Assign the policy as collateral to secure a bank loan. This is not a disposition — no tax triggered. The policy's cash value continues to grow tax-deferred while the loan is outstanding, potentially growing faster than the interest rate. Can be structured with no repayment until death, maximizing cash flow flexibility.
💰 The Collateral Loan Advantage
While the loan is outstanding, the policy still grows on a tax-deferred basis. If the growth rate exceeds the loan interest rate, the net return is positive. Business owners access liquidity with zero tax trigger, while the policy's full coverage remains intact.

🏛️
Hold in a Holding Company (Holdco)
Corporate-owned life insurance cash values are not creditor-protected from the operating company's creditors. Best practice is to hold the policy in a holding company (Holdco) rather than the operating company (Opco) to isolate the cash value from potential creditor claims.
📊
Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) Impact
Policy cash values are passive assets. If they represent more than 10% of the corporation's fair market value, shareholders may be disqualified from using the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (~$1.25M in 2024) when selling corporate shares. Advance planning is essential.
08 / Funding Structure

Three Funding Paths:
Which Is Most Tax-Efficient?

There are three ways to structure corporate funding of a life insurance policy, each with very different tax efficiency:

3️⃣
Path 1 (Least Efficient): Corporate Dividend to Owner
Corp → pays dividend to owner → owner pays personal tax → owner pays premium → personally owned policy

Requires surviving double taxation. Highest tax friction of all three options.
2️⃣
Path 2 (Middle): Increased Salary to Owner
Corp → increases salary to owner → owner pays personal income tax → owner pays premium → personally owned policy

Also requires personal-level taxation. Inefficient compared to direct corporate ownership.
1️⃣
Path 3 (Most Efficient ⭐ Recommended): Corp Pays Directly
Corp → pays premium directly → corporately owned policy

No personal-level tax required. Uses low-rate corporate funds only. Maximum tax efficiency.
